Air Gap Diffusion Distillation Research

Water-Energy Lab | Summer 2024

SolidWorks LabVIEW Heat Transfer Experimental Design

Overview

Developed a membrane-free desalination system using Air Gap Diffusion Distillation (AGDD). Unlike traditional membrane distillation, AGDD eliminates fouling by using direct evaporation across a static air gap, making it ideal for high-salinity applications with minimal maintenance.

Designed and tested a lab-scale prototype with optimized thermal-hydraulic performance, achieving efficient vapor transport and heat recovery through innovative condensation plate geometry.

Key Results

Built a functional AGDD prototype to evaluate polymer materials under realistic operating conditions. The system successfully demonstrated membrane-free desalination and provided a controlled environment for the durability study.

Design Innovations

Ridge-Enhanced Condensation Plate

  • Designed surface features that disrupt laminar film flow and promote dropwise condensation
  • Increased effective surface area while maintaining uniform condensate drainage at 45° angle
  • Minimized thermal resistance on cold side, improving overall heat transfer coefficient

Integrated Heat Recovery System

  • Hot saline flows down evaporator surface as thin liquid film
  • Water evaporates directly into air gap; salt precipitates on evaporator surface
  • Vapor condenses on cold plate, collected as purified water
  • Cold feed flows through backside channel, capturing latent heat and preheating incoming feed

Thermal-Hydraulic Analysis

The AGDD system presents a coupled heat and mass transfer problem: vapor flux depends on temperature gradients, which depend on heat flux, which depends on vapor flux. This circular dependency requires iterative numerical solution.

Mass Transfer Across Air Gap

Vapor flux is driven by the partial pressure difference between hot and cold surfaces:

J = Km · (Phot - Pcold)
where: Km = (DWA · Mw)/(δgap · Ru · Tavg)
Where: J = mass flux [kg/m²·s]
Km = mass transfer coefficient
DWA = diffusion coefficient of water vapor in air
Mw = molar mass of water vapor
Phot, Pcold = vapor pressures (calculated via Antoine equation)
δgap = air gap thickness (~3-5 mm range tested)
Ru = universal gas constant

Vapor pressures are highly temperature-dependent and calculated using the Antoine equation:

P(T) = exp(A - B/(T + C))
Antoine Constants for Water: A = 23.1963, B = 3816.44, C = -46.13
T in Kelvin, P in Pascals
For saline water, vapor pressure is reduced by salinity factor f(S)

Heat Transfer Balance

Heat leaves the hot film through two parallel mechanisms:

Qtotal = Qconduction + Qlatent
Qtotal = kair · A · (Thot - Tcold)/δgap + J · A · ΔHvap
Critical Design Tradeoff: Reducing the air gap increases conductive heat transfer, which reduces the temperature difference between hot and cold sides. If the gap is too small, both sides approach the same temperature and vapor flux becomes negligible. If the gap is too large, energy efficiency suffers. The gap must be large enough to maintain sufficient temperature gradient for phase change while minimizing thermal losses.

Iterative Solution Method

The system is solved by discretizing the module into nodes along its length:

  1. Start at inlet with known Thot,in and Tcold,in
  2. At each node, guess Tcold at condensation plate surface
  3. Calculate vapor pressures using Antoine equation: P(T, S)
  4. Calculate mass flux J and heat fluxes Qevap, Qcond
  5. Check energy balance: Qevap ≈ Qcond
  6. If not converged, adjust Tcold and repeat
  7. Update film temperature, mass flow, and salinity for next node
  8. March downstream until reaching outlet

Design Optimization Findings

  • Air gap thickness: Tested 3-5 mm range to balance vapor transport vs thermal insulation
  • Operating temperature: 90-95°C maximizes vapor pressure driving force
  • Direct evaporation advantage: Eliminates temperature polarization seen in membrane systems
  • Ridge-enhanced condensation: Improved drainage and heat transfer efficiency

Polymer Durability Study

Problem: Salt Scaling in Thermal Desalination

Salt precipitation on heat transfer surfaces reduces efficiency and requires frequent maintenance. We evaluated eight polymer materials (Acrylic, Polyethylene, PTFE, PEEK, PVDF, Polysulfone, Polycarbonate, plus Aluminum baseline) to identify surfaces that resist salt scaling.

Experimental Method

  • Measured initial contact angle and surface roughness
  • Exposed samples to saturated NaCl solution at 80°C for 6 hours (45° angle)
  • Quantified salt deposition by mass change
  • Descaled samples using DI water with stirring (400 RPM, 1 hour)
  • Measured post-cleaning surface properties

Key Findings

  • Worst performer: Acrylic - Highest mass increase due to polar ester groups attracting water/ions and porous surface structure increasing deposition area
  • Best performers: PTFE and Polyethylene - Minimal salt adhesion due to extremely low surface energy
  • Surface roughness: Acrylic and Polyethylene showed largest texture increase; PTFE and Polycarbonate maintained smooth surfaces

Recommendation: PTFE is the optimal material for AGDD evaporator surfaces, offering both low salt adhesion and easy cleaning. Polyethylene provides a low-cost alternative for applications where budget is constrained.
