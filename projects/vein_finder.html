Smart Vein Finder Imaging System

Fall 2025 (Create X Startup Class)

Med Tech PCB Design (KiCad) NVIDIA Jetson Computer Vision Embedded Systems Hardware Sourcing

Project Overview

As part of Georgia Tech's Create X startup class, our team (two CS, one BME, and myself as hardware lead) is developing a low-cost, intelligent vein finder. Unlike existing devices that provide a simple 2D map, our solution uses multi-spectral imaging to estimate vein depth and, crucially, to improve accuracy across all skin tones.

The Problem & Customer Discovery

From our customer discovery, we learned that the #1 flaw in current vein finders is their "one-size-fits-all" approach. They provide no information on vein depth or usability, and their single-wavelength NIR design works poorly on patients with darker skin tones. This leads to failed sticks, patient discomfort, and wasted time.

Our Value Proposition:

  • Depth Estimation: Provide a "recommendation" (e.g., green, orange, red) based on calculated vein depth and diameter, not just visibility.
  • Skin Tone Inclusivity: Use multiple wavelengths to eliminate the disparity in performance between light and dark skin.

The Multi-Spectral Solution

The core of our device is based on the physics of light absorption. Hemoglobin in the blood absorbs Near-Infrared (NIR) light. However, the ideal wavelength is a trade-off:

  • Shorter Wavelengths (~755nm): Hemoglobin absorption is highest, but the light scatters easily and doesn't penetrate deep.
  • Longer Wavelengths (~850nm+): Penetrates deeper and works better on darker skin, but water in the tissue also begins to absorb and scatter the light.

By capturing three separate images at 760nm, 860nm, and 940nm, our software can compare the relative vein diameter and visibility in each shot to build an approximate depth model, a task a single-wavelength device cannot do.

My Role: Hardware Lead & PCB Design

As the hardware lead, I was responsible for sourcing all components and designing the core electronics. This included selecting a See3CAM_CU135M 4K monochrome camera for its high sensitivity to IR light and designing a custom PCB in KiCad to drive the system.

The PCB's primary job is to power and control three separate banks of high-power LEDs. It uses LDD-350L constant-current drivers to ensure each LED bank (4x 760nm, 4x 860nm, 3x 940nm) receives a stable current.

Engineering Challenge & Solution

A key challenge was finding a robust way for the Jetson Nano to control the LED drivers. An initial design used a tiny I2C PWM-generator IC, but this chip proved to be a major failure pointâ€”it would constantly overheat or be destroyed by static.

The V2 design is far simpler and more robust: the Jetson's GPIO pins now directly toggle the DIM (enable) pin on each LDD driver. This allows the software to perform high-speed toggling of the LEDs with minimal, reliable hardware, removing the delicate IC entirely.

Software & Analysis Pipeline

My hardware works in a high-speed loop with the Jetson Nano's software, which is being developed by the CS members of the team:

  1. Capture: The Jetson toggles the 760nm LEDs via my PCB, captures a frame, then repeats for 860nm and 940nm.
  2. Analysis: A "hair mask" segmentation code is used to isolate only the veins. The algorithm then compares the vein's outer diameter in each of the 3 images to get relative depth information.
  3. Recommendation: A linear approximation model generates a "recommendation" map, coloring veins Green (best), Orange (okay), or Red (worst).
  4. UI Livestream: Finally, the system turns on the 850nm LEDs for a stable live view and overlays the color-coded recommendation map directly on top for the user.
